As a quant, the goal isn't just to find a "signal," but to exploit a repeatable statistical anomaly. Mean reversion works best when you can identify price exhaustion—points where the market has stretched too far from its "fair value" and liquidity providers are likely to step in to push it back.

For this setup, I’ll focus on the ES (S&P 500 E-mini) and EUR/USD. These are highly liquid instruments where institutional "rebalancing" often creates mean-reverting behavior.

The Strategy: The "Statistical Ribbon"
We will use a Multi-Timeframe (MTF) approach because mean reversion is dangerous if you fight a higher-timeframe trend. We want to catch the "snap-back" within a broader range or a cooling trend.

Strategy Components
Instrument: ES (S&P 500 Futures) or EUR/USD.

Indicator 1: Bollinger Bands (20-period, 2.5 StdDev). Standard 2.0 is too noisy; 2.5 identifies true statistical outliers.

Indicator 2: Relative Strength Index (RSI - 14-period). Used strictly as a momentum exhaustion filter.

Timeframes: 1-Hour (H1) for the "Anchor" trend and 5-Minute (M5) for the execution.

Buy and Sell Conditions
Long Condition (The Snap-Back)
H1 Context: Price must be trading within the Bollinger Bands (not "riding" the bands downward), suggesting the macro sell-off is slowing.

M5 Execution: 1. Price closes below the Lower Bollinger Band (2.5 SD).
2. RSI is below 30 (Oversold).
3. The Trigger: A single M5 candle closes back inside the Lower Band.

Target: The 20-period Moving Average (the "Mean").

Short Condition (The Mean Rejection)
H1 Context: Price is not in a vertical "moon mission" breakout; it's trading within the upper H1 band.

M5 Execution: 1. Price closes above the Upper Bollinger Band (2.5 SD).
2. RSI is above 70 (Overbought).
3. The Trigger: A single M5 candle closes back inside the Upper Band.

Target: The 20-period Moving Average (the "Mean").

The Theory: Why This Has Positive Expectancy
This strategy relies on the Law of Large Numbers and Liquidity Gaps.

Mean Reversion as Physics: In liquid markets like ES or EUR/USD, price is a function of supply and demand. When price moves 2.5 standard deviations away from the mean, it represents an extreme move that usually exhausts the immediate "market-moving" orders.

The "Rubber Band" Effect: By waiting for the candle to close back inside the band, we aren't "catching a falling knife." We are waiting for the first sign that the aggressive side has run out of steam and the counter-party (the mean-reverters) has taken control.

Timeframe Synergy: By checking the H1 chart, we ensure we aren't trying to mean-revert a market that is undergoing a fundamental regime shift (like a crash or a massive news breakout), which is the number one killer of mean-reversion quants.

Risk Management Note
Mean reversion has a high win rate but can have a "fat tail" risk (one big loss wiping out many wins). To maintain positive expectancy, a hard stop-loss must be placed at the recent swing high/low or at 3.0 Standard Deviations.
